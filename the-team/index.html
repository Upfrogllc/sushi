Captain & Owner

Charley Pereira

From the Docks of Coconut Grove

Charley Pereira's story begins on the docks of Coconut Grove, Florida. At eight years old, the local fishermen noticed a kid who wouldn't leave the marina. They built him a trailer for his bicycle so he could haul ice from the tackle store. Occasionally, they'd take him offshore for the day. That was the spark — a calling that would shape the rest of his life.

He earned a Maryland Senatorial Scholarship and an aeronautical engineering degree from Embry-Riddle Aeronautical University. He interned as a flight-test engineer at Gulfstream Aerospace, then took a job at the National Transportation Safety Board in Washington, D.C., as a black-box accident investigator — analyzing flight data recorders from crash sites.

But the ocean never let go. While working at the NTSB, Charley took on nights and weekends — side jobs, commercial fishing, anything to save enough money to buy his first boat. At 27, he did. He named it Sushi, after the restaurants in the D.C. and Baltimore area where he sold his fish commercially. He earned his USCG Captain's License in 1999.

The Outer Banks

In 2005, Charley left the NTSB and moved to the Outer Banks of North Carolina to build boats and fish full-time. He founded Island Boat Works in Wanchese, NC — a custom boat building and refit company — and designed and built the 57-foot Sushi himself with his team. A vessel purpose-built for offshore tournament fishing: twin CAT C32 engines, ZF transmissions, 55 mph top speed, 820 gallons of fuel, and the teak salon and cockpit that became the boat's visual signature.

Loss, Faith & Purpose

In 2010, Charley and Lisa lost their ten-month-old daughter, Savannah Caroline Pereira, to a tragic accident involving a baby monitor cord. Savannah was conceived through fertility treatments and was, in Charley's words, "our source of happiness."

Rather than retreat into grief, Charley became a national advocate for child product safety. He testified before ASTM International standards committees, and his work contributed directly to new federal safety labeling requirements for baby monitors (ASTM F2951). He and Lisa established the Savannah Memorial Fund through the Cade Foundation, which has raised over $142,000 to provide $10,000 fertility treatment grants to families who can't otherwise afford them. To date, the fund has supported nine families and there are now eight "Savannah Grant babies."

When asked about his secret to success on the tournament circuit, Charley's answer is consistent: his faith, his family, his mates, and the long list of mentors who shaped him.

“I have a practice of not getting off the boat and not standing and watching the scales like the other guys do. I turn away from the scales and I pray.”

A Winner Never Stops Trying

In 2021, Team Sushi won the White Marlin Open with an 85.5-pound white marlin worth $3.2 million. In 2023, they won the 65th annual Big Rock Blue Marlin Tournament with a 484.5-pound blue marlin worth $2.7 million. No other boat has ever won both.

With over $7 million in total tournament earnings and more than 10 wins or placements across the East Coast tournament circuit, Charley and the Sushi continue to fish from Pirate's Cove Marina in Manteo, NC (September through June) and White Marlin Marina in Ocean City, MD during tournament season.
